# RESEARCH

# Research Report: Educational Story Themes for School Builder

---

## 1. Research Question

What educational science topic should drive the narrative and question content for the School Builder game — nosebleeds, airplane turbulence, or another phenomenon-based approach?

---

## 2. Options Considered

| Option | Description | Educational Value |
|--------|-------------|-------------------|
| **A. Nosebleeds** | Why blood vessels in the nose rupture, prevention strategies | Human biology, cause-and-effect, health literacy |
| **B. Airplane Turbulence** | Atmospheric science, fluid dynamics, weather patterns | Physics, meteorology, real-world application |
| **C. Phenomena-Based Mixed Approach** | Multiple everyday mysteries woven into the story | Broader coverage, sustained engagement, aligns with modern science education standards |

---

## 3. Comparison and Tradeoffs

### Option A: Nosebleeds
**Pros:**
- Relatable — most kids have had one
- Simple cause-effect chain (dry air → fragile vessels → bleeding)
- Connects to health/prevention habits kids can actually use

**Cons:**
- Narrow scope — hard to build 60 diverse math questions around one topic
- Slight "gross factor" might distract younger learners
- Limited story potential after initial explanation

### Option B: Airplane Turbulence
**Pros:**
- Exciting — planes, weather, physics
- Rich science: thermals, wind shear, jet streams, fluid dynamics
- Can tie math to altitude, speed, temperature differentials

**Cons:**
- Abstract for kids who haven't flown
- Harder to visualize without animations
- May feel disconnected from "school building" theme

### Option C: Phenomena-Based Mixed Approach
**Pros:**
- Each chapter/level explores a different mystery (nosebleeds, turbulence, why ice melts, why the sky is blue, etc.)
- Math questions can vary by topic — measurement for weather, fractions for recipes, geometry for buildings
- Aligns with NGSS (Next Generation Science Standards) phenomena-driven learning
- Keeps content fresh across 60 questions

**Cons:**
- More content to create upfront
- Requires cohesive narrative thread to tie disparate topics together
- Slightly more complex game structure

---

## 4. Risks and Gotchas

| Risk | Impact | Mitigation |
|------|--------|------------|
| Single-topic fatigue | Kids lose interest after 20 questions on the same subject | Use Option C or rotate sub-topics within a theme |
| Age-appropriateness | Turbulence physics may be too advanced for Grade 3 | Tier explanations by difficulty level |
| Theme disconnect | Science topics feel shoehorned into "school builder" mechanic | Frame it as "building a school of curiosity" — each building unlocks a new mystery |
| Accuracy | Oversimplifying science leads to misconceptions | Keep explanations tight but correct; cite real mechanisms |

---

## 5. Recommendation

**Go with Option C: Phenomena-Based Mixed Approach**

Here's the narrative frame:

> **"The Curiosity Academy"** — You're building a school where each new building unlocks a science mystery. The Library teaches why we get nosebleeds. The Observatory explains turbulence. The Greenhouse covers why plants grow toward light. Each building has 5 levels, each level has math questions themed to that mystery.

This gives you:
- **12 buildings** (matches your 12 shop items — each item unlocks/upgrades a building)
- **5 questions per building per difficulty tier** = 60 questions naturally
- **Replayability** — kids want to unlock the next mystery
- **Educational depth** — not just math, but math *in context*

For the immediate build, I recommend:
1. **Nosebleeds** — Biology Wing (Grade 3-4 level, addition/subtraction questions about blood cells, healing time, humidity percentages)
2. **Turbulence** — Sky Lab (Grade 5-6 level, multiplication/division questions about altitude, wind speed, temperature changes)

Start with these two as your proof-of-concept buildings. Expand to 10 more topics once the mechanic is validated.
